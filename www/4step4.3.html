Different deterioration mechanisms exhibit varying rates of initiation and growth. For example, the crystallization of salts occurs rapidly (Godts et al. 2021) whereas the initiation and growth of mould fungi is much slower (Viitanen 2008). Therefore, it is important to use an appropriate time resolution during hygrothermal simulations to capture the events of interest. The time resolution does not only apply to the resolution of the simulation output, but also on the climate conditions. The standard EN 15026 dictates that at least an hourly resolution is to be used. However, according to Blocken and Carmeliet (2007), arithmetic averaging of 10-minute wind and rain data towards hourly values leads to the loss of information on the co-occurrence of these climate variables. The latter could lead to an underestimation of the wind-driven rain loads and intensities up to 45%. For the superior level, a time interval of at least 10 minutes should be used for the climate date, whereas hourly data is sufficient for meeting the advanced and minimum requirements in the case degradation models do not dictate otherwise.
